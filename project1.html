YORUBA CUISINE

Rich, spicy, and deeply rooted in tradition, the food of the Yoruba people of southwestern Nigeria reflects both culture and community. Centered on staples like yam, cassava, beans, and maize, it often comes alive through soups and stews such as egusi, ewedu, and gbegiri, paired with beloved swallow foods like amala, iyan, or eba. Distinctive ingredients such as palm oil, chili peppers, and iru give the cuisine its bold flavor and aroma, making every meal not just nourishment, but a celebration of heritage.


Amala with Ewedu and Gbegiri

A beloved combo across southwestern Nigeria: soft, stretchy amala (yam flour swallow) paired with silky ewedu (jute leaves) and creamy gbegiri (bean soup). It is hearty, comforting, and perfect for a main meal.




Tools

For Amala:

Pot, wooden spatula/turning stick, white nylon (for wrapping)

For Ewedu:

Small pot, ijabe or blender, spoon.

For Gbegiri:

Medium pot, blender, spoon


Ingredients (3–4 servings)

Amala
  • • 3 to 3½ cups water2 cups yam flour (elubo)
  • • 3 to 3½ cups water

Ewedu

  • • 1 large bunch ewedu leaves (jute), stripped from stems and rinsed
  • • Tiny pinch potash/kaun or a tiny pinch baking soda (helps the leaves break down)
  • • 1 teaspoon locust beans (iru), rinsed
  • • 1 tablespoon ground crayfish (optional)
  • • Salt and ground pepper, to taste
  • • ½ to 1 cup water

>Gbegiri

  • • 1 cup peeled brown beans or black-eyed beans (skins removed)
  • • 3 to 4 cups water
  • • 2 tablespoons palm oil
  • • 1 tablespoon ground crayfish (optional)
  • • 1 teaspoon ground dried pepper or to taste
  • • 1 teaspoon locust beans (iru) (optional)
  • • Salt or seasoning to taste

For serving (optional)

Cooked meat or fish

A spoon or two of simple pepper stew for topping


HOW TO PREPARE

1. Cook the Gbegiri
  • • Rinse peeled beans, add to a pot with 3 to 4 cups water, and boil until very soft and breaking apart, about 40 to 60 minutes. Top up water as needed.
  • • Mash the beans with a whisk, wooden spoon, or blend briefly until smooth. Return to the pot if you blended.
  • • Stir in palm oil, ground pepper, crayfish, and iru. Add salt or seasoning to taste.
  • • Simmer on low 8 to 10 minutes. Add a splash of water if it gets too thick. Keep warm.

2. Make the Ewedu
  • • Add ½ to 1 cup water to a small pot, bring to a boil, then add a tiny pinch of potash or baking soda. Use only a tiny amount.
  • • Add the ewedu leaves. Cook 2 to 3 minutes until softened and bright green.
  • • Blend briefly or whisk vigorously with the traditional ijabe to get a silky texture.
  • • Stir in iru, crayfish, salt, and a little pepper. Simmer 1 to 2 minutes. Keep warm.

3. Prepare the Amala
  • • Bring 3 to 3½ cups water to a rolling boil in a sturdy pot.
  • • Reduce the heat to low. Sprinkle in yam flour while stirring quickly with a wooden spatula to avoid lumps.
  • • Keep turning until smooth and stretchy. If too thick, add a little hot water and turn again.
  • • Cover and steam on very low heat 2 to 3 minutes, then turn once more and mold into portions.

4. Serve
  • • Ladle gbegiri into a bowl, add ewedu on top or beside it, and finish with a spoon of pepper stew if using.
  • • Add your meat or fish.
  • • Serve with warm amala. Pinch a bit of amala, scoop the soups, and enjoy.

Tips
  • • If you cannot find ewedu, baby spinach will work, though the texture is different.
  • • Use potash sparingly. A tiny pinch is enough.
  • • Thickness is personal. Adjust with small splashes of hot water.


HAUSA CUISINE

Hausa cuisine is rich, hearty, and deeply rooted in tradition, reflecting the agricultural lifestyle of the Hausa people in Northern Nigeria and parts of West Africa. It features grains like millet, sorghum, and maize, often prepared into thick porridges or dumplings, served with flavorful soups and stews. Meals are usually spiced with local seasonings, complemented by fresh vegetables, meats, and legumes, making the cuisine both nourishing and filling.


Tuwo Shinkafa

A smooth, slightly sticky rice dough used like a swallow. Eat by tearing a bit, rolling with your fingers, and dipping into soup.




Ingredients

  • • 2 cups short-grain rice (or 2 cups rice flour)
  • • 4–5 cups water (adjust)
  • • Salt, a pinch
  • • 1 tsp butter or oil (optional)

Tools

Heavy pot, wooden spoon or spatula, blender or potato masher (if using cooked rice)


Steps (Method A: from cooked rice)

  • • Rinse rice, then cook in 4 cups boiling water until grains are extremely soft and falling apart (about 25–30 min).
  • • Drain most of the water, leaving a little. Mash thoroughly with a potato masher or blend into a smooth, thick porridge.
  • • Return mash to low heat and stir vigorously with a wooden spoon until it forms a smooth, firm dough that pulls away from the pot (5–8 min). Add butter/oil if using and a pinch of salt.
  • • Wet your hands with cold water, shape into balls or domes. Serve hot with soups.

Steps (Method B: from rice flour, quicker)

  • • Mix 2 cups rice flour with 2½ cups hot water to form a batter.
  • • Cook over medium heat, stirring constantly until it thickens into a smooth, stiff dough (6–10 min).
  • • Shape and serve as above.

Miyan Kuka

A distinctive, slightly grainy green soup made with powdered baobab leaves (kuka). It’s savory, aromatic, and pairs beautifully with tuwo.




Ingredients

  • • 1–1.5 liters meat or chicken stock (use stock from cooked meat)
  • • 150–200 g cooked meat/chicken (cut into pieces)
  • • 3–4 tbsp kuka (baobab leaf powder)
  • • 1 small onion, chopped
  • • 1–2 tsp ground pepper or to taste
  • • 1 bouillon cube or salt to taste
  • • 1–2 tbsp palm oil (or vegetable oil)
  • • Dawadawa or groundnuts for extra flavor (optional)

Tools

Pot, ladle, small bowl for making slurry, whisk or fork



Steps

  • • Cook meat (if not already): simmer meat with onion, salt and a little pepper until tender; reserve the stock.
  • • Heat a little oil in the pot, fry a bit of chopped onion until soft (optional).
  • • Bring stock to a gentle boil. Lower heat so it’s very hot but not rolling.
  • • In a small bowl, mix kuka powder with a little warm water to make a smooth slurry (this helps avoid lumps).
  • • Slowly pour the slurry into the hot stock while stirring/whisking continuously. Kuka thickens quickly — stop when you reach desired consistency.
  • • Add cooked meat, bouillon/salt and pepper. Simmer 2–4 minutes — do not overcook (kuka can become stringy).
  • • Stir in a tablespoon of palm oil at the end for shine and flavor. Serve hot with tuwo shinkafa.

Miyan Taushe

A thick, slightly sweet and nutty pumpkin soup made with pumpkin (or butternut), palm oil and often groundnut paste.




Ingredients

  • • 500–700 g pumpkin or butternut, peeled and diced (or blended)
  • • 300–400 g meat (beef/chicken) or smoked fish, pre-cooked
  • • 1 medium onion
  • • 2 tomatoes (or 1 small tin blended tomatoes)
  • • 1–2 scotch bonnet/pepper to taste
  • • 2–3 tbsp groundnut paste (optional) or peanut butter (for body)
  • • 3–4 tbsp palm oil
  • • 1 bouillon cube/salt
  • • Handful of spinach/ugu or bitter leaf (washed)
  • • Dawadawa, carrot cubes, potatoes (optional)

Tools

Pot, blender or sieve, wooden spoon


Steps

  • • Cook the meat in salted water until tender; reserve the stock.
  • • Boil or steam pumpkin until soft, then mash or blend into a smooth puree. If using fresh tomatoes and pepper, blend them together.
  • • In a pot, heat palm oil, fry chopped onion until translucent. Add blended tomato + pepper and fry until oil separates (5–8 min).
  • • Stir in groundnut paste (diluted with a little hot stock if thick) and cook for 2 min to reduce raw taste.
  • • Add pumpkin puree, some reserved meat stock (start with 2 cups), and bring to a gentle simmer. Adjust thickness with more stock or water.
  • • Add cooked meat (or smoked fish), bouillon/salt, and simmer 8–12 minutes so flavors meld.
  • • Add chopped greens near the end and cook 2–3 minutes until wilted. Taste and adjust seasoning.
  • • Finish with a dash more palm oil for color and aroma. Serve with tuwo shinkafa.

Quick serving tips

  • • Serve tuwo hot; it’s easiest to roll by wetting fingers.
  • • Miyan Kuka is thin-to-medium consistency; Miyan Taushe is thicker and richer — both work great together.
  • • Substitute: if kuka powder isn’t available, try a spinach/green-leaf soup instead; if no palm oil, use vegetable oil (flavor differs).
